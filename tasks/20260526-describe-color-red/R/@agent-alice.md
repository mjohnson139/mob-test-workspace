## Q1 [researcher]: What is red physically? (wavelengths, light spectrum, physics of color perception)

Red occupies the longest wavelength range in the visible light spectrum, roughly 620–750 nanometers, just before infrared. When light in this range strikes the retina, it preferentially activates the L-cone photoreceptors (long-wavelength sensitive), which the brain interprets as the color red. This detection mechanism is why red is the last color lost at twilight — lower-energy photons in this range require stronger cone activation thresholds than shorter wavelengths like blue or green.

The physics of red also explain phenomena like Rayleigh scattering: because red's longer wavelength scatters least in the atmosphere, it passes through more air mass efficiently. This is why sunsets appear red/orange — sunlight traveling through more atmosphere loses its shorter wavelengths first, leaving red visible at low solar angles. It is also why danger signals, traffic lights, and brake lights use red: it penetrates fog and haze better than shorter-wavelength colors.

## Q3 [researcher]: What psychological and emotional effects does red have on people?

Red has documented physiological arousal effects: exposure to red environments measurably increases heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration speed compared to neutral or cool colors. This makes red effective for stimulating appetite (used widely in fast food branding) but counterproductive for environments requiring calm focus or sleep. The arousal response appears to be partly innate — studies show infants respond to red with more agitation than other colors before cultural conditioning could explain it.

Psychologically, red creates a dual association with threat and reward that mirrors its biological roots. Red signals danger (blood, fire, predators) triggering vigilance, but also signals ripeness in fruit and sexual attraction in many primates, triggering approach behavior. In competitive contexts, wearing red is associated with a statistically significant advantage in close contests — studies of Olympic combat sports found red competitors won more often, and researchers attribute this to both actual physiological effects on the wearer and subtle dominance signals sent to opponents.
